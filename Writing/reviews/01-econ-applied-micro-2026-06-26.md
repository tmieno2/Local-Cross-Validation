# Referee report: A New Approach to Model Selection Based on Local Economically Optimal Input Application Rates

## Recommendation: Reject and resubmit (major revision required before the paper is reviewable)

The paper makes a sensible and useful point: when the objective is a profitable input recommendation, models should be selected by a decision-relevant loss (EONR / profit) rather than by yield prediction accuracy. I am sympathetic to the message and the simulation results are suggestive. However, the manuscript in its current state is not a publishable AJAE article for three independent reasons, each of which I consider blocking. First, the central idea is an instance of a broad and well-developed principle (the gap between predictive accuracy and decision quality) that the paper presents as new without engaging the relevant literature, so its genuine contribution, the operationalization for an unobservable EONR, is neither isolated nor defended. Second, the claims ("substantially more effective," "substantially reduces profit loss") rest on a single simulation design with one crop, one truth functional form, one variogram, and no inference whatsoever, and the paper does not scope its conclusions to that evidence. Third, the manuscript is not self-contained or legible enough for a general readership: prices are never given, the abstract metadata field and several citation keys are broken, candidate-model tuning is undescribed, and the typo/inconsistency density is at the level an editor would return for copyediting before review. Below I separate blockers from major and minor issues.

## Findings

### Blockers

1. **[blocker] Novelty is overstated; the general principle is uncited.** (Introduction, lines 72-79; Conclusion, lines 936-941.) The paper frames "select by decision-relevant loss, not predictive accuracy" as a new idea specific to nitrogen/corn. This is a well-known general principle in applied economics and ML: prediction-policy problems, decision-focused / loss-calibrated / task-based learning, and the estimation-vs-policy-objective distinction. None of this literature is cited; the only conceptual antecedents invoked are precision-ag papers (Kakimoto et al. 2022; Morris et al. 2018). As written, an AJAE editor would read the core claim as reinventing a known idea in a narrow setting. Action: explicitly position the contribution as the *operationalization of decision-relevant model selection when the decision target (EONR) is unobservable* (the GAM-proxy local-EONR criterion under spatial CV), cite the general decision-focused/prediction-policy literature, and state plainly what is new relative to it. If the contribution is the proxy construction, say so and defend it as the contribution.

2. **[blocker] Claims of effectiveness outrun the evidence.** (Abstract, lines 22-26; Introduction, lines 77-79; Conclusion, lines 917-924.) Every comparative claim ("substantially more effective," "substantially reduces profit loss," "far better") is supported by exactly one DGP: one crop (corn), one truth functional form (quadratic-plateau), one variogram (spherical, range 600m, zero nugget), one price pair, one experimental design, five hand-chosen candidate models, and no measure of sampling uncertainty (no standard errors, confidence intervals, or tests on any reported difference across the 500 iterations). The "Was our approach just lucky?" section (lines 853-875) varies only the spatial-correlation strength, not the functional form, crop, price, or model set, so it does not establish robustness to the dimensions that matter most. Action: (a) state the single-DGP, no-inference scope as an explicit limitation up front, not buried; (b) attach uncertainty to the headline numbers (e.g., distribution / CI of the profit-loss difference across iterations, or a paired test); (c) soften "substantially/far more effective" to claims proportionate to one design, or add designs (alternative truth functional forms, prices, variograms) before making generality claims.

3. **[blocker] Not self-contained: prices never stated.** (Equation 2, line 176, defines profit using crop price $p$ and N price $w$; the EONR closed form, lines 404-410, and all profit equations, lines 490-515, depend on $P_C$, $P_N$.) The numerical values of the corn price and nitrogen price are never given anywhere in the text. Profit loss in \$/acre (Figure 5, the paper's headline economic result) cannot be interpreted or reproduced without them. The true-EONR range (113-273 kg/ha) is also a function of these prices. Action: report $P_C$ and $P_N$ (values, units, year/source) in the data-generation section.

4. **[blocker] Broken metadata and citations; copyediting state below submission threshold.** The abstract appears as body text with no abstract field populated (lines 5-28; the rubric flags the empty abstract metadata field). At least five citations render as unresolved keys: `frank1990comparison?` (line 172), `graler2016spatio?` (line 193), `dietrich1996fast?`, `wood1994simulation?` (lines 194-195), `bullock2019data?` (line 210), `zhao2021causal?` (line 420). These are core methodological and calibration references and none appear in the reference list. Combined with the typo density (item 9), the manuscript is not in reviewable condition. Action: populate the abstract field, resolve all citation keys, and add the missing references.

### Major

5. **[major] Definitional inconsistency in the independent variables.** The covariate vector is defined as the 12-element $X_j = (\beta^{1,1}_j, \ldots, \eta^{2,1}_j, \eta^{2,2}_j)$ (line 328) and training is said to use "$N$ and $X$" (line 368). But line 362-363 states "the same values of ($N_j$, $\alpha_j$, $\beta_j$) are used as the independent variables," and the causal-forest section writes the heterogeneity function as $\theta_k(\alpha_j, \beta_j)$ and $g(\alpha_j, \beta_j)$ (lines 452-458) even though the displayed equation uses $\theta_k(X_j)$ (line 448). A careful reader cannot tell whether the models see the 12 decomposed covariates, or $(\alpha_j, \beta_j)$. Since $\alpha$ and $\gamma$ are explicitly said *not* to be in $X_j$ (line 331), the references to $\alpha_j$ as a regressor are internally contradictory. Action: pick one notation ($X_j$) and use it everywhere; correct the CF text.

6. **[major] "Correct selection" frequencies are not defined precisely enough to interpret the central tables/figures.** (Section 3.1, lines 696-731; Figure 4; Table 2, not rendered.) The headline metric, how often each approach "correctly" selects the best model, hinges on what "true best model" means, and the manuscript defines "best" circularly: Step 2-1 (lines 351-355) says the best model is judged by how its estimated VR EONR compares to the *true* optimal N, but Figure 4's caption (lines 711-713) defines "actual best" as "lowest RMSE of site-specific EONR prediction," while the profit-loss results (lines 750-763) rank by profit. EONR-RMSE-best and profit-best need not coincide. Because the two accuracy tables did not render (flextable), the reader is asked to trust in-text frequencies that depend on this undefined criterion. Action: state one explicit definition of "true best model" and use it consistently across Step 2-1, Figure 4, Table 2, and Figure 5; ensure the tables render.

7. **[major] Candidate-model tuning is not described, which undercuts the fairness of the comparison.** (Section 2.2, lines 412-481.) RF, BRF, and CF performance is highly sensitive to hyperparameters, and the paper's claim that yield-based selection "consistently favors models with poor EONR performance" (BRF selected ~all rounds) could be an artifact of untuned or differently-tuned candidates. The text says hyperparameters are tuned "via cross-validation" in the intro (lines 56-57) but the Method never describes the tuning grid, criterion, or whether tuning itself used yield or EONR loss (which would be circular). Action: describe the tuning procedure for each candidate model, the search space, and the objective used for tuning.

8. **[major] Numerical inconsistency in the headline BRF result.** The abstract/Section 3.1 text says yield-based selection "selected BRF for all the 500 simulation rounds" (lines 699-700); Table 2 text says BRF was chosen "for all the simulation rounds" but "correct only 60 times" (lines 728-729); Section 3.1 text says BRF "was actually the best model in only 61 rounds" (line 700). Figure 4 (which I inspected) shows the Yield bar for BRF at roughly 494, not 500, and the "True" bar for BRF near 60. So three different numbers (60, 61) appear for "BRF is truly best," and "all 500" conflicts with the figure's ~494. Action: reconcile these counts and make the figure and text agree exactly.

### Minor

9. **[minor] Pervasive typos and malformed sentences.** Examples: "substracted" (line 220), "calcurate" (line 611), "multia-arm" (line 444), "liner model" (line 726), "There interaction terms" (line 386), "the field was into 8 (= 4 × 2) blocks" (missing "divided", line 228), "selected its abilities" (line 58, missing "on"), "estimted" (line 357), "seleted" (Figures 5 and 8 captions), "associate" (line 499), "its was possible" (line 276), "real-world limitations" (singular/plural, line 278), double "the the" (lines 541, 570, 632), stray comma "employed with both approaches,." (line 526). For an AJAE submission this density is a copyediting blocker; I list it as minor only because it is mechanical. Action: full copyedit pass.

10. **[minor] Audience fit: the economic question is buried under precision-ag machinery.** (Method occupies lines 81-690; the economic framing is two paragraphs.) For a general AJAE readership, the lead should foreground the economic point (aligning the selection criterion with the decision objective, and the twist that the decision target is unobservable) before the OFPE plot/subplot/cell geometry. Roughly half the Method is field-design detail that a general reader does not need to grasp the contribution. Action: tighten the data-generation exposition and elevate the economic framing earlier.

11. **[minor] Equation 6 has an internal inconsistency.** (Lines 314-317.) The decomposition is written $\eta = \min(\eta^1,\eta^2)$, $\eta^1=\min(\eta^{1,1},\eta^{1,2})$, then $\eta^2 = \min(\eta^{2,1} + \eta^{2,2})$, which mixes "+" inside a min of a single argument. Action: correct to the intended form.

12. **[minor] The RMSE-of-yield aggregation formula sums rather than averages across folds.** (Line 579: $RMSE_{Yield_m} = \sum_{f=1}^F RMSE_{Yield_{m,f}}$.) The text immediately above (line 575-576) says "the average RMSE for yield prediction is calculated," but the displayed equation is a sum, not a mean (no division by $F$), unlike the local-EONR RMSE (line 619) which does divide. Action: fix to a mean if that is what was used.

13. **[minor] "Local EONR" is described inconsistently as the validation target.** (Lines 593-600 vs 642-654.) The GAM produces a single "uniform (not variable) EONR" per fold/test region, called "local EONR," and candidate models are scored on how their *average* site-specific EONR ($\hat N^*_{f,m}$) matches that scalar (line 612, 619). This means a model could match the local mean while getting the spatial pattern wrong, yet the paper's value proposition is site-specific (variable-rate) recommendation. The text should acknowledge this aggregation mismatch as a limitation of the criterion, not only as a feature. Action: discuss why matching a local mean EONR is an adequate proxy for selecting among variable-rate models.

14. **[minor] $\theta_k(X_j)$ as both an estimand and a yield difference is loosely stated.** (Lines 464-468.) The identity $y_j|_{N_k}-y_j|_{N_1}=\theta_k(X_j)$ treats a realized yield difference as equal to the treatment-effect function, ignoring the error term that appears in the CF model on line 448. Minor, but a careful reader will notice. Action: write in expectation.
