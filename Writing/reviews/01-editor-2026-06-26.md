## 3. Frame-level conflicts (surfaced, NOT resolved — for Taro to arbitrate)

These are genuine disagreements about what the paper is and whether a feature is fatal. I lay out each side; I do not split the difference.

### 3.1 Is the uniform-EONR validation target a workable proxy or a fatal identification flaw?

1. **The conflict.** All three referees notice the same structural fact (Section 2 consensus #2): the criterion scores a candidate model's *fold-averaged* site-specific EONR against a GAM's *single uniform* fold-level EONR. They disagree sharply on severity.

2. **The "fatal / central methodological claim not supported" side (econ-econometrics-ml #1, blocker).** Treats this as textbook aggregation bias that undercuts the paper's core: the criterion measures the conditional mean of EONR over the fold, not the conditional-on-X EONR that drives profit, so a model can match the fold mean while getting the variable-rate map (the entire economic content) wrong. Demands either a demonstration that fold-mean matching is monotone in true profit loss (a rank/correlation statistic across iterations) or a replacement criterion using within-fold heterogeneity. Under this framing the central claim is not supported by the criterion that implements it.

3. **The "workable, note as a limitation" side (econ-production-ag #13 minor; econ-applied-micro #13 minor).** Treats the same fact as a clarification-and-caveat item: explain why matching the fold-level mean is the right (or adequate) target for selecting a variable-rate model, and acknowledge the aggregation as a limitation rather than redesigning the criterion. Under this framing the proxy is a defensible engineering choice that needs better defense in text, not a flaw that invalidates the results.

4. **What is at stake.** If econ-econometrics-ml is right, the revision requires a new validation (monotonicity of RMSE_LE in profit loss, or a heterogeneity-aware criterion) and the headline may not survive. If the production-ag/applied-micro read is right, the revision is largely expository plus a robustness check. This is the single most consequential arbitration in the round and it determines the scale of the revision.

### 3.2 Is the contribution genuinely novel, or a known idea applied narrowly?

1. **The conflict.** Both econ-applied-micro and econ-production-ag flag novelty, but frame the comparison set differently, which changes how damaging it is.

2. **The "known general principle, currently overstated" side (econ-applied-micro #1, blocker).** The "select by decision-relevant loss, not predictive accuracy" idea is a well-developed general principle (prediction-policy problems, decision-focused / loss-calibrated / task-based learning). None of that literature is cited; only precision-ag antecedents (Kakimoto et al. 2022, Morris et al. 2018) appear. An AJAE editor would read the core claim as reinventing a known idea in a narrow setting unless the contribution is explicitly repositioned as the *operationalization for an unobservable EONR*.

3. **The "real but under-delineated against the nearest precedent" side (econ-production-ag #8, major).** Focuses on Kakimoto et al. (2022), which the paper itself cites for the premise that yield accuracy does not imply EONR accuracy. The marginal contribution is the operational selection criterion, and the task is to state what is new versus Kakimoto and the OFPE-economics literature (de Lara et al. 2023, Gardner et al. 2021, Li et al. 2023).

4. **What is at stake.** The two reviewers point to different literatures the paper must engage (general decision-focused/ML vs precision-ag/OFPE-economics). The arbitration is whether positioning against the broad prediction-policy literature is required (applied-micro's bar) or whether sharpening against Kakimoto and OFPE economics suffices (production-ag's bar). Both agree the *fix* is to reframe the contribution as the operationalization; they disagree on how wide the citation net must be cast.

### 3.3 Does the "intentionally misspecified" quadratic candidate plus SE-favoring DGP bias the test, or is it a neutral design choice?

1. **The conflict.** econ-econometrics-ml (#7, major) and econ-production-ag (#7, major) both argue the DGP may pre-determine the winner; the authors (in text) assert the misspecification does not compromise the test "because our objective is to test the selection method."

2. **The "not neutral" side (both referees).** The DGP is quadratic-plateau with strong spatial correlation; SE is the most efficient quadratic estimator under that structure; the LEONR criterion's entire demonstrated advantage is that it "responds to the subtlety of the statistical advantage SE brings." So the misspecified parametric form and the spatial DGP jointly determine the winner the criterion is credited with finding. econ-production-ag adds that the covariate decomposition (additive tau/beta vs nested-min eta) builds the relative difficulty of each model into the DGP and that SE being "true best" in 412/500 runs may be an artifact.

3. **The authors' implicit position (as quoted by referees).** Misspecification is acceptable because the goal is to test the selection method, not the agronomic model; correctly-specified quadratic-plateau was skipped on numerical-instability grounds.

4. **What is at stake.** Whether the headline generalizes ("substantially more effective" as a property of EONR-based selection) or is specific to an SE-favoring DGP and a partly redundant model set. The referees want either a correctly-specified-candidate robustness run or an explicit bound on how much the result depends on the misspecification. This is a generality question Taro must scope.

## 5. Verifier integration — ground-truth correctness (URGENT, separate category)

The verifier ran in author-side mode and is the **only** input that could compare manuscript numbers against the code/results. In manuscript-only mode, **no referee could have caught any of the following.** These are "the number is wrong" or "the number is stale," which is categorically different from a referee's "cannot check / under-specified." Headline tally: **19 match, 11 do-not-match, 3 cannot-verify, 5 stale.** The reassuring news first: the entire main Table 2 and no-sp Table 3 bodies, all inline `r num_*` selection counts (61, 412, 381, 317, 93, 8, 327, 60), and the GAM-accuracy description all reproduce exactly. The problems cluster in two regions.

### 5.A Does-not-match / stale — must be corrected as factual errors

1. **Section 3.3 fold-repeat percentages are wrong throughout (verifier F1-F8).** None of the six cited LEONR percentages match the data; the **"7 folds, 5 repeats" case does not exist** (only 5/10/20 folds were run — 62.4% actually belongs to 20 folds/5 repeats); the "consistently increasing trend" narrative is not supported by the recomputed values; and the yield-based range is **12.0-13.0%**, not the stated 14.2-14.4%. Correct LEONR values per the verifier: 5f1r 60.2, 5f5r 67.6, 5f10r 64.6, 10f1r 59.6, 10f5r 65.4, 10f10r 68.2, 20f1r 59.0, 20f5r 62.4, 20f10r 68.0. **The entire paragraph must be regenerated from the .rds objects.** (This directly undercuts econ-econometrics-ml #3's reading of a "consistent improving trend" — but note the referee was reasoning from numbers that are themselves wrong; the monotonicity claim fails for a more basic reason than MC error.)

2. **"Yield-based selected BRF for all 500 rounds" is false in the main case (verifier D9).** Data show BRF = 495, LM = 5; stated twice (ln 699, 728) and contradicted by the paper's own Table 2 cell "60 (495)". It IS exactly 500 in the no-sp case (E5). Reconcile to "nearly all (495)." (This intersects econ-applied-micro #8, which flagged the 500-vs-~494 inconsistency from the figure alone; the verifier confirms 495 is the truth.)

3. **Section 2.1 calibration figures are stale, written against an earlier data-generation configuration (verifier A9, A10, B1, B2).** Current data have **5** trial rates (avg ≈ {75, 115, 154, 194, 234}), mean yield ≈ **13,458 kg/ha**, and true EONR spanning ≈ 82-210 (cell-level) / 124-164 (field means). The manuscript's "six rates {80,130,154,184,219,270}", "11,416 kg/ha", and "113-273" do not match any of these. (This intersects econ-production-ag #5 and the broad "realism" concern, but is sharper: the descriptive calibration numbers are simply from a superseded data version.)

4. **Trial-rate construction text contradicts code (verifier A8).** Manuscript says 20 kg/ha subtracted from the low rate; code uses `Nk_minus = 40` (the +20 high side matches).

5. **"Selected SE as often as it was selected as the actual best" is overstated (verifier D3).** 381 (LEONR-selected SE) vs 412 (SE true best), off by 31; reword to "almost as often" or give both numbers.

### 5.B Cannot-verify from saved data (note, do not conflate with 5.A)

6. **Error-term mean ε = 1,370 kg/ha could not be recomputed (verifier B3)** — ε is not stored as a named column and generation uses `pasim` internals not in the repo. This is distinct from the referees' separate objection (econ-production-ag #5) that 1,370 as a *mean* of a mean-zero error is physically impossible and is likely a standard deviation. So this item has two independent problems: the referee's logical/typographical flag AND the verifier's inability to confirm the value at all. Both should be resolved when Section 2.1 is regenerated (5.A.3).

### 5.C Important distinction for the author

7. **Prices: the verifier corrects a likely misframing (C1).** Prices ARE defined in the code (pCorn ≈ 0.246 $/kg, pN ≈ 2.205 $/kg). The referee blocker (Section 4, item 1) is therefore a *reporting* gap, not a missing model input — the values exist and need only be stated and (per econ-production-ag #2) varied. The Tables 2/3 that referees could not see (consensus #13) DO match the data per the verifier, so the rendering failure is a presentation bug, not a sign the numbers are wrong.
