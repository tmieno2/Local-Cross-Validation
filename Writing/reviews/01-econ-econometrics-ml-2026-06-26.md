# Referee report: econ / econometrics-ml

## Recommendation: Major revision (bordering on reject on the central identification point)

The paper makes a sensible high-level argument: when the decision object is a site-specific EONR map, models should be selected on EONR-relevant accuracy rather than yield-prediction accuracy. But the proposed operationalization has a structural mismatch between what it validates against and what it claims to validate. The selection criterion `RMSE_LE` compares each candidate model's *fold-averaged site-specific EONR* to a GAM-estimated *single uniform local EONR* on the held-out fold. Matching a fold mean does not test a model's within-fold heterogeneity, which is the entire economic content of variable-rate management, so the criterion can reward a model that gets the field-average right while getting the variable-rate map wrong. Compounding this, the GAM proxy is itself estimated and the authors' own scatter (Figure 6) shows a systematically biased lower cluster plus a broad band of under-estimation at high true EONR, with none of this error propagated into the selection conclusions. Finally, every comparative claim ("substantially more effective," profit-loss distributions, 57%-to-66% success rates) is reported without any Monte Carlo standard error or test, so the reader cannot tell whether the LEONR-vs-yield gap or the fold/repeat ranking is real or noise. The idea is publishable; the current execution does not yet support the claims.

## Findings

1. **[blocker] The selection target is a uniform fold-level EONR, but the estimand of interest is the site-specific EONR map. The criterion may validate the wrong object (Method, "Model selection based on local EONR," lines 585-621; `RMSE_LE` formula line 619).**
   `RMSE_LE` is built from `(N*_{f,m} - N*_{f,gam})^2`, where `N*_{f,m}` is the *average* of the candidate model's site-specific EONRs over the test fold (Step 5, line 611) and `N*_{f,gam}` is a *single uniform* EONR from a GAM that deliberately excludes the soil covariates (lines 635-640). Two models with identical fold means but very different within-fold dispersion score identically on `RMSE_LE`, yet they imply different variable-rate maps and different profits. This is textbook aggregation bias: the criterion measures the conditional mean of EONR over the fold, not the conditional-on-X EONR that drives profit. The paper needs to either (a) demonstrate that fold-mean matching is sufficient for ranking variable-rate maps (e.g., show `RMSE_LE` is monotone in the true profit loss `Delta-pi` across iterations, with a correlation/rank statistic), or (b) replace the criterion with one that uses within-fold heterogeneity (e.g., shrink fold size so each "local" region is more homogeneous, or validate against a covariate-conditional GAM EONR). As written, the central methodological claim is not supported by the criterion that implements it.

2. **[blocker] The GAM proxy's estimation error is systematic, not just noisy, and it is neither propagated nor bounded. (Results, "Accuracy of Local EONR estimation with GAM," lines 774-792; Figure 6, g_gam_leonr.png.)**
   The text describes "two clusters" that "over- and under-estimate" and then asserts "the vast majority of the data points are located around the 1-to-1 line ... indicating the usefulness." The figure does not support that benign reading. There is a distinct lower cluster where true LEONR is roughly 60-100 but GAM estimates 125-200 (a 50-100+ unit over-estimate), and across the main mass the cloud sits *below* the 1-to-1 line at high true EONR, i.e., GAM systematically under-estimates large true EONRs. Systematic proxy bias does not average out; it can tilt selection toward whichever candidate shares GAM's implicit smoothing/under-response behavior (a circularity risk, since GAM is itself a flexible no-covariate response curve much like the way RF/BRF smooth). The authors must (i) quantify the bias (mean error, RMSE, slope of true-on-estimated, by cluster), (ii) characterize what field conditions produce the lower cluster, and (iii) show selection conclusions are robust to it (e.g., re-rank using a debiased proxy, or report how often the proxy bias flips the selected model). Absent this, "the usefulness of GAM-based estimation" is an unsupported claim about the paper's load-bearing component.

3. **[blocker] No uncertainty quantification anywhere; comparative conclusions could be within Monte Carlo error. (Abstract line 22-28; Results lines 696-763; Conclusion lines 917-924; Figures 4, 5, 7, 8.)**
   With 500 iterations the paper reports point frequencies (e.g., LEONR correct 327 vs yield 60) and mean/distributional profit losses but no Monte Carlo standard errors, no confidence intervals on the success rates, and no paired test that the LEONR criterion beats the yield criterion per iteration. The fold/repeat success rates (57.2%, 58.4%, 62.4%, 65.8%, 59.4%, 65.2%; lines 825-833) are interpreted as a "consistent improving trend," but with MC SE on the order of sqrt(0.6*0.4/500) ~ 2.2 percentage points, several of these differences (e.g., 57.2 vs 58.4, 65.8 vs 65.2) are plausibly noise, and the claimed monotonicity in folds/repeats is not established. Every comparative claim, including "substantially more effective" (Abstract line 22; Intro line 78; Conclusion line 922), needs a reported MC standard error and an appropriate paired test (the two criteria are evaluated on the same 500 fields, so a paired comparison of per-iteration profit loss is available and should be reported with its SE/CI).

4. **[major] Spatial-CV leakage is plausible and not ruled out; the effective number of independent comparison units is very small. (Method, "Model Selection," lines 522-540; Figure 3, g_split_example.png; `RMSE_LE` averages over only F folds.)**
   The variogram range is 600 m (line 198) on a field 432 m x 864 m; that range is comparable to the field's own dimensions, so essentially all of the field is within one correlation length. Figure 3 shows test folds as contiguous vertical strips whose boundaries directly abut training cells, with no buffer/exclusion zone. Train and test plots straddling a fold boundary therefore remain strongly spatially correlated, which inflates apparent out-of-fold performance and can bias the ranking. The text gives no buffering, no statement of how cluster boundaries relate to the 600 m range, and no leakage check, so the spatial CV is not replicable-as-correct from the text alone. Separately, `RMSE_LE` reduces each model to F scalar comparisons (10 in the main case): a 10-point RMSE built from a biased proxy is a thin and high-variance basis for ranking five models, which is exactly why the fold/repeat sensitivity (Section 3.3) matters and why item 3's uncertainty quantification is essential. Please add (a) a buffer zone between train and test and a leakage diagnostic (e.g., residual semivariogram across fold boundaries), and (b) justification that F scalar contrasts are a stable ranking basis.

5. **[major] The causal-forest EONR lives on a 5-point grid and is not reconciled with the continuous EONR of the other candidates, biasing the horse race. (Method, "Causal Forest (CF)," lines 431-481.)**
   CF discretizes continuous N into the 5 trial levels, recasts the problem as 4 multi-arm contrasts vs the lowest rate, and restricts the EONR to "N_k that produces the highest positive profit differential" (lines 479-481). The LM/SE/RF/BRF candidates produce continuous EONRs (closed form or numerical argmax). A grid-restricted EONR is mechanically coarser and will differ from the continuous true EONR (and from the GAM proxy's continuous EONR) for reasons unrelated to model quality, so CF is handicapped in both the `RMSE_LE` and the profit comparison by construction. This is visible in Figure 4: CF is essentially never the true best and almost never selected by either criterion. The paper must either put CF on comparable footing (finer treatment grid, or interpolate the treatment-effect curve to a continuous EONR) or explicitly acknowledge that the discretization, not CF's heterogeneity estimation, drives its poor showing. Relatedly, the text never states whether honest splitting / out-of-bag (honest) trees, sample splitting, or any tuning was used for CF (or for RF/BRF); with manuscript-only access, the CF specification is not replicable.

6. **[major] The candidate field is not a fair, informative horse race: RF appears to be a degenerate candidate and the contest collapses to SE vs BRF. (Results, Figure 4 g_ranking.png; "True model performance," lines 698-705.)**
   In Figure 4 RF is never the true best model and is essentially never selected by either criterion (its bars are at zero). With one candidate inert, two parametric variants (LM, SE) that share the same misspecified quadratic estimating equation, and CF handicapped by discretization (item 5), the effective comparison is SE (true best 412/500) vs BRF (yield's automatic pick). The strong headline result ("yield-based selects BRF 500/500, correct only ~60 times") then largely reflects that the data-generating spatial structure strongly favors SE, which the SE-aware LEONR criterion recovers. The authors should explain why RF is dominated, confirm RF/BRF tuning was non-degenerate, and discuss how sensitive the headline contrast is to the particular (small, partly redundant) model set. As it stands, the generality of "substantially more effective" is overstated relative to a five-model field that is effectively a two-model field.

7. **[major] The "intentionally misspecified" quadratic LM/SE candidates interact with the SE-favoring DGP in a way that is not neutral to the test. (Method, "Linear and spatial error models," lines 370-399; Conclusion claims of generality lines 936-941.)**
   The authors argue (lines 388-393) that using a quadratic rather than quadratic-plateau form does not compromise the test "because our objective is to test the selection method." That argument is only valid if misspecification is symmetric across candidates and uncorrelated with which criterion wins. Here the DGP is quadratic-plateau with strong spatial correlation, the SE model is the most efficient quadratic estimator under that spatial structure, and the LEONR criterion's whole demonstrated advantage (Section 3.4, lines 853-875) is that it "responds to the subtlety of the statistical advantage SE brings." So the misspecified parametric form and the spatial DGP jointly determine the winner the criterion is being credited with finding. The paper should show the result holds when the parametric candidates use the correctly specified quadratic-plateau form (the authors cite numerical instability, lines 389-390, but instability is an implementation issue, not a reason to skip the robustness check), or at minimum bound how much the headline depends on the quadratic misspecification.

8. **[minor] Figure 6 / proxy claim wording overstates what the scatter shows. (Lines 783-786.)**
   "the vast majority of the data points are located around the 1-to-1 line ... indicating the usefulness of GAM-based estimation" is not defensible from a plot with a visibly biased secondary cluster and a high-EONR under-estimation band. Replace the qualitative claim with quantitative fit statistics (overall and per-cluster) and soften the conclusion accordingly (ties to item 2).

9. **[minor] Notation/specification inconsistencies make the method hard to verify from the text. (Multiple locations.)** Examples: line 363 and lines 452-458 say the covariates used are "(alpha_j, beta_j)" or "(N_j, alpha_j, beta_j)," but the constructed covariate vector X_j (line 328) contains the beta-, tau-, and eta-split variables, not alpha or the true beta; alpha and gamma are explicitly excluded (line 331). The CF treatment-effect functions are written as theta_k(X_j) in one place (line 466) and theta_k(alpha_j, beta_j) in another (lines 452-458). Equation 6 (line 316) mixes min(.,.) and "+" inside the same min for eta^2. The yield-RMSE aggregation (line 579) sums fold RMSEs without dividing by F (it is labeled "average"). Several missing citation keys render as raw `(**frank1990comparison?**)`, `(**graler2016spatio?**)`, `(**dietrich1996fast?**)`, `(**wood1994simulation?**)`, `(**bullock2019data?**)`, `(**zhao2021causal?**)` (lines 172, 193-195, 210, 420). These need to be fixed before the method can be judged replicable.

10. **[minor] Tables 2 and 3 did not render and could not be assessed. (Lines 733-744, 877-890.)** The two accuracy tables are flextable objects absent from the rendered text, so the per-cell correct/selected counts (e.g., the SE 317/381 and LM 8/93 figures quoted at lines 724-728) could not be verified against a rendered table. For a methods paper, these tables carry the core quantitative evidence and must render, ideally with MC standard errors per item 3.

## Bottom line for the editor
The contribution rests on (a) the LEONR proxy identifying the site-specific EONR target and (b) the comparative result surviving uncertainty. Item 1 (aggregation mismatch) and item 2 (systematic, unpropagated proxy bias) put (a) in doubt, and item 3 (no MC error anywhere) leaves (b) unestablished. Items 4-7 mean the demonstrated advantage may be specific to an SE-favoring DGP and a partly degenerate model set rather than a general property of EONR-based selection. None of this is fatal to the idea, but the current draft cannot support its central claims without substantial methodological additions.
